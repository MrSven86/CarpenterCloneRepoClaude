## Conversion Heuristic

**Conversion Heuristic**

**C = 4M + 3V + 2I − F − 2A**

-   **M** = Motivation (highest weight --- if motivation is strong
    enough, people tolerate friction)

-   **V** = Value proposition force

-   **I** = Incentive

-   **F** = Friction

-   **A** = Anxiety

If perceived value = 5 but negative elements = 7, the result is −2.
Conversion will be low unless motivation compensates.

**Cost Force VS Value Force**

**Cost = Friction + Anxiety**

### Friction.

Definition: Psychological resistance

Friction is anything that creates impatience for the Prospect when
interacting with your company and or website. Basically anything that
doesn't immediately makes sense. Anything that makes me have to "work".
Friction can be long loading pages. Friction can be text walls. Friction
can be bad UX, like - "where is the phone number, or contact page?"

**Anxiety:** Anything that raises doubts and concerns for the Prospect.
Can this company be trusted? Are the claims true? What will happen once
I submit my information? What will they do with my information? (This
can be mitigated by *trust icons*, 3:d party reviews or by *process
level value proposition*).

### Value

**What is value?**

Value = Appeal + Exclusivity that is Credible (believed).

**Appeal:** I want this (it's attractive, it makes us salivate).

**Exclusivity:** I want this from you - 'I can't get it just like this
anywhere else'.

**Credibility** = I believe this. To succesfully convey value you need
to be believed. That's why credibility is key. You can't just make any
claim, it needs to be believed. Credibility is achieved by:

**We achieve credibility and make our messaging more effective by:**

**Specificity** - substitute general description with specific facts.

**Quantification** - support your message with numbers and data

**Verification** - customer reviews

**Believable**: And by explaining the offer with as much text as is
required, using a language the prospect understands. Simply saying
something which you the company understands is valuable, might not make
any sense to the prospect, and if the prospect doesn't understand why
something is valuable, it won't have any positive impact on him/her.

**Velocity**

**Velocity:** "You don't want bounce rate - you want motion down your
page" FM. The velocity increases by lowering cost and increasing value.

In fact, the goal of any business, or any page is to lower cost and
increase value. That is called increasing Velocity. This is the key to
everything, the most fundamental concept.

### Clarity & Believability

What can I do here? What do you offer, what can I do, why should I do
it? Engage me in a conversation explaining those things, using benefit
oriented credible language

A website needs to achieve two main movements:

1.  I understand it.

2.  I believe.

If I don't understand it, nor believe it, it's like multiplying your
value with 0. 100 x 0 is still 0. 

People cannot believe until they understand - step 1\] I understand,

**Do not make claims -- foster conclusions**

Every single action you want someone to take is preceded by a decision,
and every decision is preceded by a conclusion. The visitor is jaded and
doesn't believe wild claims. Use inference -- present key evidence, to
foster a conclusion.

### Customer Centric rather than Company Centric

**Company Centric Logic VS Customer Centric Logic**

You want to build out the website and your value proposition focusing on
"the other", rather than yourself - talk to the prospect about what
he/she wants, not what you want them to do. Focus on what they get
rather than ordering them to submit or fill out forms. "You catch more
flies with honey than with vinegar." A common key mistake is using
company centric logic over customer centric logic.

### The 4 types of Value Propositions

There are 4 types of Value Propositions

**Primary level value proposition** - why You and not anyone else?

**Product level value proposition** - a particular offering (must
harmonize with primary value proposition)

**Process level value proposition** ("plvp") - the value proposition for
each of the micro asks that move people up your funnel (classic example
of this is a button that says 'click now' - a plvp should add value and
inform what happens when you click 'click now to get..xyz' eg instant
access - a promise, and a process. Each button is a door to a room. Plvp
= Show what's hiding behind the door in a way which promises sonething
(related to your primary value proposition).

Process level vp can be as easy as asking: What do I get in exchange for
clicking your button?

**Prospect level value proposition** - sometimes you need to know
exactly who you are talking to and guide him or her in a special way.

**+ Category level value proposition** (talking about the actual
business category rather than your specific offering).

**The types of value proposition and the website**

A great website usually has all of these - primary, product (note:
sometimes the company only has a single product or often a page should
focus on a single offering, the 70% rule), process, prospect and
category level value proposition.

The point is to know when to focus on what level of value proposition.
Around a cta, you want to focus on **process level value proposition** -
what happens next. On a friendly introductory text to your product or
service or company, there might be need for **prospect level value
proposition**. Next to the cta, as a reminder of your **primary value
proposition** it might be appropriate **with process level value
proposition**. And typically at the top of your front page, and in key
areas of your pages, it is appropriate with **primary level value
proposition** or **product level value proposition** on a special
product page, or if your company is associated with a single product or
service.

**A note on "Category level" value proposition**

### Only Factor

**- Be remarkable in one crucial aspect of your business**

To occupy space in someones mind, to have the customer allocate a slot

need to narrow your focus to at least a subset of a market. Focus
increases the connectivity of your brand, a unique focus is powerful.

Seth Godin calls it remarkable, Jim Collins simply says 'be the Best
in.. (your category)', Peter Thiel says, be 10x better. They are all

Exclusivity: Only with us, only with this, only here can you achieve X

If you're selling strawberries from Chernobyl, chances are your not

### Evidentials

Use key facts to **infer**, or foster a conclusion

Wild claims are not believed; back up your claims with evidentials.

**Don't make claims about yourself, let others make claims about you**

The most powerful evidentials are typically not what you, the company
says, but what OTHERS say about you. Examples: 3:d party evidentials,
such as proven reviews, awards, certifications and other type of proof
such as "join 30 00 others" or "100 000 meals served", certified
service, etc. This is the most powerful type of content, because it is
believed. What others say about the company trumps what the company says
about itself times x1000.

### Sequence 

Where are they in the journey? Are they ready to get started? If they

Say the right thing at the right time.

**"You don't optimize the page, you optimize the Thought Sequence of the

##  The Value Proposition

The website/business should have a single strong purpose, and everything
on the website should be geared towards that purpose. That single,
strong purpose should be your companies **Value Proposition**.

Why do your company even exist? This is the justification

An offer with high Appeal and Exclusivity, conveyed with clarity and
credibility is your value proposition. Appeal/Exclusivity = "Only
factor"

The force of your Value Proposition is a mix of the appeal of your offer
and the exclusivity of the offer.

A strong value proposition should naturally lead the customer to draw
four conclusions:

1\. Appeal -- "I want this."

2\. Exclusivity -- "I want this from you---I can't get it just like this
anywhere else."

3\. Credibility -- "I believe this."

4\. Clarity -- "I understand this."

Avoid wild claims---they are not believed. The proposition should be
clear, credible, and believable.

**The formula:**

The Value Proposition is a claim, an "Ultimate Reason", backed up by
clear, undeniable evidentials, answering:

*"If I .. (am your ideal prospect)"*

*"Why should I ..(buy from you, rather than any of your competitors)?"*

The answer should be structured exactly like this:

*"Because.., Only with (us) will you get X that allows you to do Y"*

## Clarity

**Clarity Over Cleverness**

**Clarity trumps persuasion.**

-   Don\'t use plays on words --- they create double meaning when you
    need clear meaning

-   Confusion = resistance. Confusion is the *inexcusable* enemy because
    it\'s always fixable

-   Vague brand names (Universal Companies, Acme, etc.) communicate

    proposition

-   Don\'t put important information in \"banner blindness\" zones (top
    strip above the fold)

### USE COMPLETE THOUGHTS! = adds clarity. Avoid: TITLES!

To have a complete thought, you need a subject and a predicate.

A sentence that is not a complete thought, and that doesn't give me a

"CDPAP - Consumer directed Personal Assistance Program"

Why it's bad: CDPAP = stops all forward momentum immediately

Confusing. If I start of confused, you're in trouble.

## Copywriting 

### General rules

**The six basic rules of good copywriting**

These basic copywriting principles might seem simple at first glance,
but pay close attention to them and don't break them! They are proven to
work again and again and are undeniable.

**Don't break these!**

1\. Right: Be clear Wrong: Be vague

2\. Right: Focus on the get Wrong: Focus on yourself

3\. Right: Be credible Wrong: Make unverifiable claims

4\. Right: Point first Wrong: Point last

5\. Right: Sequence information Wrong: Ask for too much too soon

6\. Right: Use language customers understand Wrong: Be technical & use
jargon

**Again, remember, conversion increases when we lower Friction and
Anxiety and increase value.**

**A note on tone**

Humanization of your copy builds trust. A human voice is warmer than a
computer voice, builds trust.

Speak normally, complete sentences, as you would if the customer came
into a store. In a store you wouldn't have 6 people running up and

### "Cognitive conditions & Treatments"

Cognitive Condition: Internal detachment

Definition:

A state of general apathy or disinterest due to a lack of relevance,
urgency and importance in call-to-action cluster elements.

Root Cause:

Commonly caused by a lack of empathy regarding the customer\'s
motivations and/or an attempt to focus too narrowly (or broadly) on a
particular offer.

Signs and Symptoms:

-   When there is only one primary CTA.

-   When the text of your CTA is too specific.

-   When the text of your CTA is too general.

-   When the text of your CTA is value neutral.

-   When the text of your CTA is value negative.

**Known treatments**

**TREATMENT #1**: Intensify the explicit or implicit value in the button
text (i.e., point-first).

**From this**

\[Button: Subscribe Now ⭐\]

**To this**

\[Button: Upgrade Now ⭐\]

RESULT: (In this experiment, focusing the button text on what the
customer got if they subscribed, an upgrade, produced 75% increase in
conversion)

**TREATMENT #2:** Select a completely different offer that is more
closely matched to customer interests.

**From this**

Complete a free online budget analysis to help us understand your unique
situation.

\[Button: Get Started Now\]

**To this**

Estimate your single, low-interest monthly payment by entering your debt
information on our secure site.

\[Button: Estimate My Monthly Payment\] 

RESULT: 125% increase in conversion. This cta is right at the epicenter
of what the visitor wants, on what matters the most to him/her.

**TREATMENT #3:** Add additional calls-to-action to reach multiple
customer types. - if it's clear, if you have more than one segment.
Otherwise it causes confusion and hurts conversion.

**Example**

**From this**

**Request more information**

Fill out the form below and a Distribution Expert will contact you to:

-   Provide pricing information

-   Discuss your unique needs

-   Answer any questions you may have

\[Button: Request More Information\]

**To this**

**Request more information**

State: Select state ▼ Annual Revenue: Select Revenue ▼

\[Button: Request More Information\]

**How much does it cost?**

offers distribution options that range in reach and price.

**Cognitive Condition: Non-sequential shock**

Definition:

A state of negative surprise due to an improperly sequenced \"ask\" in
the marketer-customer dialogue.

Root Cause:

Commonly caused by the lethal assumption that the customer is further
(or not far enough) along in the thought sequence than they truly are.

Signs and Symptoms:

-   The CTA asks for a significant commitment rather than a small step.

-   The CTA is positioned on page extremities (top, bottom or sides).

-   The CTA produces a high degree of friction (e.g., includes form
    fields).

**TREATMENT #1:** Aligning the CTA more logically to the thought

**TREATMENT #2:** Relocating the CTA to different places on the page.

**Cognitive Condition: Compositional paralysis**

**Definition:**

A state of confusion that halts the momentum of the prospect caused by
the design of choices relative to the surrounding elements on a page.

**Root Cause:**

Commonly caused by an attempt to accomplish too many company objectives
with a single page.

**Signs and Symptoms:**

-   When the CTA is not visually distinct from the rest of the page.

-   When there is a high degree of difficulty-oriented friction on the
    page.

-   When the CTA does not fall naturally into the main eye-path of the
    offer.

**TREATMENT #1:** Reducing the number of CTA options on the page.

**TREATMENT #2:** Adding contrast to the CTA relative to the rest of the
page.

**TREATMENT #3:** Moving the CTA into the main eye-path of the page.

\"The marketer must engage the prospect in a mental dialog and guide
each step, minimizing any source of friction. **The marketer makes
meaning; the prospect makes decisions -- never confuse the two
roles.**\"

"Long copy is not the problem, confusion is the problem. Enough copy is
the amount of copy it takes to establish with clarity enough value to
outweigh the perceived cost".

"If you give me too many options your conversion suffers". Simplify.

\"There are two flawed \"asks\" that will mitigate the power of our
message: (1) an \"ask\" to the wrong person, (2) an \"ask\" at the wrong
time. **Doing the right thing is more important than doing the thing
right.** The marketer must deliver the right message to the right
prospect at the right time -- or it is no longer the right message.\"

**ASK: WHat is the prospect REALLY after? Then Provide THAT in the CTA**

**Friction & Anxiety Checklist**

**Friction** = anything that creates resistance or cognitive effort:

-   White or colored text over busy background images --- hard to read

-   Vague CTAs (\"Let\'s talk about it\" --- what happens when I click?)

-   Two equally weighted options competing for attention

-   No headline --- just a title

-   Logos or branding that don\'t communicate what you do

-   Playing a video before establishing any context

**Anxiety** = fear or uncertainty about what happens next:

-   \"Register\" as button copy --- nobody woke up wanting to register

-   \"Free\" sounds free to the visitor but raises questions: What\'s
    the catch? Will I be upsold?

-   Asking for commitment before establishing value

-   Video with no description --- How long? What\'s it about? How loud
    will it play?

**A note on layout friction**

*If there are similar looking paths heading to different parts of the
website, and there's no information, or weight (indicated by thing such
as shape, size, color or movement), as to which is more important, it
creates unecessary friction. Should I click the blog, the article pages
or the about us page?*

*You'd rather have one message at a time, flowing vertically. (it can
still have two columns, and have some horizontal layout, but it
shouldn't say two things at once).*

**Reducing Friction**

Design decision

Have a clear eye path. Typically with one element at a time flowing
vertically, not horizontally so the eye is not diverted to two different
messages. Control the eye path by highlighting the most important stuff
(CTA:s). Don't highligt too much! Control the eye path lead it to the
most important stuff using shape, color, size or movement.

*If you have to have two paths next to each other, indicate which is
more valuable by highlighting its shape or color.*

Have light text, max 3 to four lines in a row, as opposed to text walls.
Use bullet points and pictures and lots of air. It should be easy to
browse.

**Layer**

***The Psychological Fulcrum: Perceived Value vs. Perceived Cost***

*Every page element sits on a fulcrum. On one side is perceived cost, on
the other is perceived value. For high performance, perceived value must
outweigh perceived cost. There are only two ways to tip that fulcrum:
add value or remove friction.*

***Process-Level Value Propositions***

*When you want someone to take an action that moves them deeper into the
funnel (fill out a form, click a button, watch a video), that action has
its own value proposition --- separate from your product\'s value
proposition.*

*A page should commit to a ratio: either 70% product value proposition /
30% process, or 30% product / 70% process. If you don\'t focus,
conversion leaks.*

***Page 1: Insurance Quote Form***

*The header \"Get Started\" implies friction and anxiety simultaneously.
It signals a big, unknown process. I don\'t know how long it takes, what
happens next, whether you\'ll email me, call me, or take me to more form
fields.*

*In most cases, anxiety and friction are caused by unanswered questions.
Does your call to action create more negative questions than it
answers?*

*Fix: rename it something like \"Quick Quote.\" Add a subheader ---
\"You\'ll have your quote within seven minutes\" or similar. Make it
feel fast, easy, low-risk.*

*The form asks for name, email, and phone number, but that\'s not enough
information to generate a real quote --- so I know something else is
coming, and it probably feels like a spam call. If you need the phone
number, justify it: \"Tell us where to send your quote\" in the email
field. For phone: \"We can text the quote to you for faster response\"
or \"In case we have critical questions.\"*

*The \"We value your privacy\" line is a good move but could be
stronger. Make it smaller, move it closer to the button, and use the
freed-up space for a credibility indicator --- a testimonial, a
five-star rating, a third-party endorsement.*

*The subheader \"All Lines Insurance has access to restaurant coverage
providers other companies don\'t\" contains a genuine differentiator,
but it\'s buried in confusing syntax. \"Has\" reads like a grammatical
error. The real question: why is that access valuable? How does it help
me? That needs to be made clear.*

***Page 2: Personal Accident Insurance***

*\"Get Affordable Personal Accident Insurance\" --- everyone says
\"affordable.\" When everybody says it, nobody\'s saying it.*

*The fix is to ask \"why\" repeatedly. Why is it affordable? Keep asking
why until you reach a concrete fact. A fact drives a conclusion, which
precedes a decision. The marketer\'s job is to foster a conclusion, not
make a claim. \"Affordable\" is a claim. A specific dollar figure is a
fact.*

*The opening paragraph says \"a simple accident like a broken bone can
cost thousands even if you have health insurance.\" That\'s obvious.
Instead, lead with specifics: \"The average person with medical
insurance spends an additional \$1,497 on a broken bone.\" Start
point-first with the main payload at the beginning of the sentence.*

*The \"Watch Video\" button has several problems. First, it looks
identical to the CTA button --- same shape, color, and styling. This is
conflicting visual code: the user can\'t tell what\'s a primary action
and what\'s secondary. Second, \"Watch Video\" is company-centric --- it
tells me what you want me to do, not what I\'ll get. How long is it?
What will I learn? Will it auto-play audio and embarrass me at work? All
those unknowns add friction. Tell me what the video delivers.*

*The \"Call\" CTA has the same problem. Don\'t tell me to call. Tell me
what the call gives me, how short it is, what questions get answered,
and why it\'s worth my time.*

*The stock photo of a guy in suspenders pointing --- it wastes space. It
doesn\'t build trust, doesn\'t make me laugh, doesn\'t help me solve my
problem. Use that space to explain the value of taking the next action.*

*The core principle: stop telling people what you want them to do. Tell
them what they\'re going to get.*

**The \"F-Pattern\" Reality**

Eye-tracking research proves that people don\'t read online, they scan.
They read the first horizontal line, drop down the left margin, read a
little across again, and then just scan the left edge. It looks exactly
like the letter \'F\'.

If your text is just a giant block, a \"wall of text\", you are fighting
human nature. High visual density triggers immediate cognitive overload.
Before the brain even processes the first word, it calculates the
\"effort\" required. If it looks like a textbook, they leave.

**How to format for the modern reader** You have to design your text,
not just write it.

-   **The 3-Sentence Rule:** Never exceed 3 sentences in a paragraph.
    White space is your best friend.

-   **The Inverted Pyramid:** Put the conclusion first. Don\'t build up
    to a grand finale at the end of a paragraph. State the value
    immediately so the F-Pattern scanners catch it.

-   **Strategic Bolding:** Bold the core concepts. A reader should be
    able to scan only your bolded text and still understand 80% of the
    article\'s value.

**Bullet Points are Mandatory:** Break up dense explanations into lists.
It immediately lowers the visual density score of the page.

**The Ideal Customer**

For whom?

Who is your ideal customer? For whom do you exist?

**The Reason**

You should rather be the best answer to a small market, then a general
reason to a large market. There should ideally be an 'only' in this
answer.

**Design**

Reduce friction in Design

**Eye path**

What is the eye path on the page? Are you guiding the customer?

Evenly weighted graphical elements and columns confuses the visitor -
where should he focus his eyes? Use single column layouts to guide the
eye path.

**Credibility - Important! Look for Value Indicators**

Is achieved through what I call "value indicators" - which as 3:d party

This is the most powerful type of content. What others say about the
company trumps what the company says about itself. If you find such copy
when going through the collateral, that should be mixed in with the copy

What would be potential claims of value one could make?

1\. reliability

2\. Lowest Rates

3\. Easy Process

4\. Brand Name

5\. Payment Plan Options

6\. Reduced Anxiety

7\. Service Quality

8\. Community Involvement

*Typically around 10 claims are enough*

**STEP #2: Estimate the Force of Each Claim**

Locate claims that are both unique and appealing

(It\'s ok to not know exactly what is appealing - make a best guess)

Eliminate claims that are neither appealing nor exclusive

Give each claim a 1-5 score for the two categories appeal and
exclusivity

Appeal 1-5

Exclusivity 1-5

*Appeal = \" want this\"*

*Exclusivity = Exclusivity - \"I can\'t get this anywhere else\"*

Once you have identified 1-3 (or more! If necessary) powerful claims of
value, Identify 2-5 evidentials for the top claim/s off value to support
it, to add credibility, clarity, believability
